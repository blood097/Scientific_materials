Kirichek, E.A., Tsyganova, A.V., Flores-Félix, J.D. *et al*. Symbiotic compatibility between *Rhizobium laguerreae* and its host. *Archives of Microbiology* 208, 91 (2026)
>The efficiency of interaction between legumes and rhizobia depends on the genotypes of both partners, which may lead to inefficient symbiosis. This study examined interactions between three pea (*Pisum sativum* L.) genotypes and six *Rhizobium laguerreae* strains classified into three genospecies (gsN, gsO, gsR) via whole-genome phylogenetic analysis. The peculiarities of interaction between each pea genotype and each strain were studied at histological and ultrastructural levels. Both normally developing symbioses and those with various disruptions in the infection process, bacterial release, differentiation of bacteria into bacteroids, and the ability of bacteroids to maintain their functional activity were identified. It was shown that cv. ‘Rondo’ was the most successful in forming symbioses with *R. laguerreae* strains, in turn, strain AMPS05 was the most effective on all three pea genotypes studied. Thus, the efficiency of interaction in symbioses formed between pea and *R. laguerreae* strains depends on the genotypes of both partners.

<br>`Pan-genome_Bradyrhizobium`

>Global food security and sustainable agricultural development are critically dependent on the continuous improvement of crop productivity and quality. Among the key natural processes supporting agricultural systems, biological nitrogen fixation occupies a paramount position. The present study focuses on elucidating the genomic diversity and symbiotic capabilities of *Bradyrhizobium ottawaense*, a nitrogen-fixing bacterium. The research centers on commercial *B. ottawaense* strain RCAM0104, aiming to decipher the genetic foundations of bacterial-plant interactions and the evolutionary adaptability of these microorganisms. Experimental assessments of symbiotic compatibility revealed that RCAM0104 exhibits effective nodulation with *Arachis hypogaea*, *Glycine soja*, and *Vigna unguiculata*, while demonstrating partial effectiveness with *Glycine max*. Histological examinations confirmed successful infection processes in most host plants, with the exception of white nodules observed in cultivated soybean. Comparative genomic analysis of closely related strains RCAM0104 and RCAM0503 identified overrepresented functional pathways associated with sphingosine degradation, leucine metabolism, and multidrug extrusion systems. These findings underscore the genetic flexibility of *B. ottawaense* and its capacity to adapt to diverse host plants. The observed host-specific nodulation patterns suggest that accessory genome elements play a critical role in determining symbiotic compatibility. This research contributes significantly to our understanding of the evolutionary dynamics and ecological plasticity of rhizobia within legume-rhizobium symbiosis, providing valuable insights into the mechanisms governing microbial-plant interactions. We anticipate that the loss of efficacy on one host may be associated with gains of other.
